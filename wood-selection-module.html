Module Introduction

Choosing the Right Wood for Greenwood Carving

The wood you choose shapes everything — how the blade moves, how the grain responds, whether the finished piece sings or fights you. Yet many carvers select wood based on whatever's available rather than what's ideal.

This module will help you identify, assess, and select the right wood species for your greenwood carving projects. You'll learn to read the characteristics that matter and match species to the work you want to do.

By the end of this module, you will be able to:

  1. Identify six common UK species used in greenwood carving by their grain, colour, and bark
  2. Explain how hardness, workability, and splitting behaviour affect carving
  3. Distinguish freshly felled (green) wood from seasoned wood and assess readiness
  4. Select the appropriate wood species for common carving projects
Estimated time: 15–20 minutes · 8 screens · Includes 2 knowledge checks
Section 1

Why Wood Selection Matters

In greenwood carving, you're working with freshly felled timber — wood that still holds its natural moisture. This is fundamentally different from dry woodworking. The species you choose determines:

Workability How easily the blade passes through. Some species carve like butter; others resist every cut.
Splitting behaviour Whether the wood cleaves cleanly along the grain or tears unpredictably — critical for roughing out blanks.
Finish quality How the surface responds to knife work. Fine-grained woods take beautiful tool finishes; coarse woods may need more work.
Drying behaviour How the piece moves as it loses moisture. Some species warp dramatically; others stay stable.

A common mistake is treating all wood as interchangeable. Choosing birch for a project that needs lime — or ash when you need cherry — means fighting the material instead of working with it.

Section 3

Properties That Matter for Carving

When assessing any piece of wood for carving, focus on these three key properties:

Hardness

How resistant the wood is to your blade. Softer woods (lime, willow) are easier to carve but may dent in use. Harder woods (ash, cherry) require sharper tools and more effort but produce durable pieces. For greenwood carving, hardness is relative — even "hard" species are significantly easier to work green than seasoned.

Workability

How cleanly the wood responds to edge tools. This depends on grain structure, not just hardness. Lime is soft and clean-cutting. Willow is soft but can be fuzzy and woolly. The best test is a single paring cut with a sharp knife — if the shaving curls cleanly, you've got good workability.

Splitting Behaviour

How the wood responds to being cleaved along the grain. This matters because most greenwood projects start with splitting a log into blanks. Ash splits beautifully along straight grain. Lime can be unpredictable. Understanding splitting behaviour saves time and waste.

Pro tip — Always test a small section first. Even within the same species, individual trees vary based on growing conditions, age, and stress. A twisted birch from a windy hillside will behave very differently from a straight one in sheltered woodland.

Section 4

Green Wood vs. Seasoned Wood

Greenwood carving relies on working wood while it still contains its natural moisture. Understanding the difference between green and seasoned wood — and knowing how to assess what you've got — is essential.

🌿 Freshly Felled (Green)

  • High moisture content (often 40–80%+)
  • Cuts easily — blade glides through
  • Shavings curl, feel damp
  • Wood feels heavy for its size
  • End grain may show visible moisture
  • Fresh scent when cut
  • Bark still firmly attached

🪵 Seasoned (Dried)

  • Low moisture (12–20% for air-dried)
  • Harder to carve — more resistance
  • Shavings are brittle, snap or crumble
  • Wood feels lighter
  • May show end-grain checks (cracks)
  • Less scent
  • Bark loose or falling away

How to Assess Freshness

When you're sourcing wood, use these quick tests:

The thumbnail test Press your thumbnail into the end grain. If it leaves an impression easily, the wood is likely still green.
The weight test Pick it up. Green wood is noticeably heavier than you'd expect for its size.
The cut test Make a small paring cut. Green wood produces curled, moist shavings. Dry wood makes dust or brittle chips.
The end-grain check Look at the ends. Deep radial cracks indicate significant drying. Surface checks only may mean it's still green inside.
Timing matters — Most species are best carved within days to weeks of felling. If you can't carve immediately, seal the end grain with PVA glue or wax and store in a cool, shaded place. Bark-on logs hold moisture longer than split billets.
